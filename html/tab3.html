What is the Radiation of the universe?

Radiation from the universe refers to the electromagnetic radiation found everywhere in the universe that originated shortly after the Big Bang, when the universe was very young and hot. This radiation is called cosmic microwave background (CMB) radiation, and it is detected in all directions of the sky. The cosmic microwave background radiation is the result of the release of photons that occurred when the universe was only a few hundred thousand years old. At that time, the universe was in a state of high energy and density, and the photons were trapped in a hot, dense plasma.

As the universe expanded and cooled, photons were released from the plasma and began traveling through intergalactic space. Today, these photons are part of the radiation background that is detected in all directions of the sky. The discovery of cosmic microwave background radiation in the 1960s was a major milestone in cosmology, as it provided strong evidence for the Big Bang theory and allowed scientists to measure density fluctuations in the early universe that gave lead to the formation of galaxies and large-scale structures.
